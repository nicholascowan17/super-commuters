Nothing "Super" About Super-Commuting

BY NICHOLAS COWAN

Nationwide, one in 36 commuters (approximately 4.2 million Americans) are super-commuters, traveling 90+ minutes to work each day. This practice has become increasingly common, but nowhere more pronounced than in the most expensive coastal metro areas: New York, San Francisco, Washington D.C., and Los Angeles. Residing in neighborhoods increasingly distant from unaffordable city centers, these workers often spend two hours or more on public transportation or battling traffic. In light of the COVID-19 pandemic, many of these individuals likely took solace in the rise of remote work; to what extent they return to their super-commutes in the future is largely unknown.

Methodology

Public Use Microdata Areas (PUMAs) are used as the geographic scale for aggregating travel-to-work data. PUMAs are statistical areas defined by the Census Bureau for disseminating American Community Survey (ACS) estimates. They have a minimum population of 100,000 and are aggregated from census tracts.

This map employs Public Use Microdata Sample data from the most recent five-year estimates (2015â€“2019) of the American Community Survey. This microdata, provided by IPUMS USA, is composed of records containing information collected on individuals and households, sampled and weighted to create accurate population estimates. Utilizing five-year estimates of the ACS helps to ensure increased statistical reliability and reasonably precise estimates by industry and PUMA.

IPUMS estimates for travel times were pulled for all PUMAs in the continental U.S. and travel mode data was pulled specifically for PUMAs in New York City, Washington D.C., San Francisco, and Los Angeles. Data was cleaned in Python 3.8 and joined to spatial boundaries in QGIS 3.18. These datasets were exported as geojsons and simplified in mapshaper to reduce file sizes.
