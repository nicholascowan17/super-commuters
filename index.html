Nothing "Super" About Super-Commuting

BY NICHOLAS COWAN

Nationwide, one in 36 commuters (approximately 4.2 million Americans) are super-commuters, traveling 90+ minutes to work each day. This practice has become increasingly common, but nowhere more pronounced than in the most expensive coastal metro areas: New York, San Francisco, Washington D.C., and Los Angeles. Residing in neighborhoods increasingly distant from unaffordable city centers, these workers often spend two hours or more on public transportation or battling traffic. In light of the COVID-19 pandemic, many of these individuals likely took solace in the rise of remote work; to what extent they return to their super-commutes in the future is largely unknown.

About

Nationwide, one in 36 commuters (approximately 4.2 million Americans) are super-commuters, traveling 90+ minutes to work each day. This practice has become increasingly common, but nowhere more pronounced than in the most expensive coastal metro areas: New York, San Francisco, Washington D.C., and Los Angeles. You will find as you toggle through the maps that commute times in rural areas rarely exceed an hour; however many urban workers far exceed those thresholds. In New York City's metropolitan area, 649,839 workers are super-commuters (6.6% of all workers); in Washington D.C. the total is 138,513 (4.2%); 115,356 (4.6%) in San Francisco; and 228,210 (3.5%) in Los Angeles. Residing in neighborhoods increasingly distant from unaffordable city centers, these workers often spend two hours or more commuting each way.

By zooming into each of these metropolises and examining their travel patterns, another fascinating discovery is made: super-commuters are far more likely to take public transit to work than the average worker. This is true for all four cities. For some, this means a lengthy trip on commuter rail each day between their suburban home and downtown workplace; for the rest who actually live within city borders (many of whom many be essential workers), this simply reflects the unreliable and inconvenient nature of many urban public transit services.

In light of the COVID-19 pandemic, many super-commuters likely took solace in the rise of remote work. It is still largely unforeseen to what extent they will return to their commutes in the future. Some may bargain with their employers, accepting a slight pay cut for the luxury of working from home, and others may move even further away from the city center, seeking larger properties at more affordable price points. Meanwhile, for the essential workers who do not have these options, transit services could become even less convenient as schedules are adjusted to accomodate the fewer commuters and reduced revenues. For cities to avoid this transit death spiral, funds need to be invested now to build back worn down infrastructure, increase transit reliability and safety, and overall attract new passengers.

Methodology

Public Use Microdata Areas (PUMAs) are used as the geographic scale for aggregating travel-to-work data. PUMAs are statistical areas defined by the Census Bureau for disseminating American Community Survey (ACS) estimates. They have a minimum population of 100,000 and are aggregated from census tracts.

This map employs Public Use Microdata Sample data from the most recent five-year estimates (2015–2019) of the American Community Survey. This microdata, provided by IPUMS USA, is composed of records containing information collected on individuals and households, sampled and weighted to create accurate population estimates. Utilizing five-year estimates of the ACS helps to ensure increased statistical reliability and reasonably precise estimates by industry and PUMA.

IPUMS estimates for travel times were pulled for all PUMAs in the continental U.S. and travel mode data was pulled specifically for PUMAs in New York City, Washington D.C., San Francisco, and Los Angeles. Data was cleaned in Python 3.8 and joined to spatial boundaries in QGIS 3.18. These datasets were exported as geojsons and simplified in mapshaper to reduce file sizes.
